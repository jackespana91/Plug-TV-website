The camera

A fixed chase view down a vanishing-point road, homage to the 1985 Paperboy cabinet. The street recedes to one point on the horizon; houses fan out as they approach. Ace is pinned to one screen position while the world rushes toward camera — he never scales, never relocates. He is seen from behind: back, shoulders, the back of his backwards cap. Any art that shows his face by default is built for the wrong camera. The only exception is a brief three-quarter head-turn in tuck and the full front-facing beat in crashed — see the pose cards.

Ace's fixed anchor — every frame, every pose, draws from this one point Road perspective (converges on the vanishing point)

Who he is

Ace, ~11 years old, scrappy and delighted — never actually scared, even mid-disaster. Backwards red cap (from behind: crown visible, brim low at the neckline). Blue windbreaker, open enough to flap at speed. Canvas newspaper bag on his right hip, diagonal strap across the back — deliberate, since he rides the right-hand lane and throws right, so bag and throwing arm agree. Red BMX, flat bar, oversized canvas pedals. Head and hands run big for his body — he reads as small as 65px tall on a phone screen, so the silhouette has to survive that before anything else.

Palette

Exact hex, pulled from the live renderer. The world outside him runs warm golden-hour light with violet shadows — never pure black — so give him a rim light from one consistent side, and keep his own contact shadow violet-tinted to match.

The eight poses

If time runs short tonight, build in this order: ride → throw → crashed → tuck carry almost every moment a player actually sees; skid, tumble next; wheelie, idle last. Every sheet: same rule — one horizontal strip, transparent background, frames cropped tight then centred and bottom-anchored so the ground-contact point never drifts between frames. The line in gold-tinted highlight is the one "hero" frame worth nailing first in each sequence.

ride

BUILD FIRST
8 framesloopspeed-synced
Full pedal-crank rotation, 45° per frame. Draw one clean neutral-speed loop — the game retimes it to match run speed. Jacket hem and bag strap should sway a touch independently of the body so the loop doesn't read as mechanical.
  1. 1Right pedal 12 o'clock, left at 6 — passing position
  2. 2Right ~1:30, power stroke begins, slight torso dip
  3. 3Right ~3 o'clock, power stroke at its deepest
  4. 4Right ~4:30, left leg beginning to rise
  5. 5Right 6 o'clock, left at 12 — mirror of frame 1
  6. 6Right ~7:30
  7. 7Right ~9 o'clock, torso rebounding upward
  8. 8Right ~10:30, approaching frame 1 — must loop seamlessly

throw

BUILD FIRST
6 frames450ms≈13fps
The single most-repeated animation in the game — every delivery, every ~1.2–1.5s. Right arm reaches to the hip bag and throws right. The paper itself flies as a separate sprite the game already animates, so the release frame just needs the arm mid-swing, not the paper leaving frame-perfectly.
  1. 1Anticipation: arm begins reaching back, torso twists
  2. 2Grab: hand at the bag opening
  3. 3Wind-up: arm drawn back and up, torso coiled
  4. 4Release — arm swung out right, paper leaving the hand
  5. 5Follow-through: arm past the release point, slight overrotation
  6. 6Return: arm relaxing to the handlebar, settling into ride

crashed / dazed

BUILD FIRST
1 frameor 3-loopfaces camera
The one pose that breaks the rear-view rule — the payoff of every loss. Ace sat up on the ground beside the toppled bike, spiral/dizzy stars, papers fluttering down, a calm half-smile that says "...again?" A static hold is enough; if you do the 3-frame loop, only the ring of stars needs to move (rotate through 0°/120°/240°) — everything else stays locked.

tuck

BUILD SECOND
5 frames~930msplays at 0.3× speed
The near-miss escape — the game's signature moment, played back in slow motion so it reads even slower on screen than the source timing suggests. The one place besides crashed where a slice of his face shows.
  1. 1Alert: shoulders rise, head begins turning
  2. 2Brace: deeper crouch, head turned further, one eye visible
  3. 3Peak tension — full crouch, eye wide, the "yikes" beat
  4. 4Relief: head turning back forward, shoulders relaxing
  5. 5Recover: back to riding posture

skid

BUILD THIRD
6 frames~900ms≈10fps
Cashing out / reaching the flag. A stop skid with rising dust, closing on a modest, pleased flourish — bigger wins already get their own confetti and fanfare elsewhere, so this frame doesn't need to carry the whole celebration.
  1. 1Brake initiated: weight shifts forward
  2. 2Skid begins: rear wheel sliding, first dust
  3. 3Skid peak — maximum lean, biggest dust cloud
  4. 4Settling: bike straightening, dust thinning
  5. 5Stopped: fully settled, upright
  6. 6Flourish: a small arm-up/fist-pump — keep it modest

tumble

BUILD THIRD
8 frames~780ms≈10fps
The wipeout. Comedic, never painful — a cartoon spill, not a crash-test. Ends directly into crashed's starting position.
  1. 1Impact: front wheel catches, bike jerks
  2. 2Launch: rider separating from the bike, both airborne
  3. 3Peak chaos — rider and bike scattered mid-air, papers flying
  4. 4Falling: descending, still tumbling
  5. 5First impact: hits ground, begins to bounce/roll
  6. 6Roll: tumbling across the ground
  7. 7Sliding to a stop: momentum bleeding off
  8. 8At rest — hands off into crashed

wheelie

BUILD LAST
7 frames700ms≈10fps
Milestone deliveries (×5/×10/×25/×50) and skateboard hops. A showoff beat, not a full stunt.
  1. 1Compression: weight shifts back, anticipation crouch
  2. 2Lift-off: front wheel just leaving the ground
  3. 3Rising: front wheel ~30° up
  4. 4Peak — front wheel at its highest, body leaned back
  5. 5Hold: sustained peak, small settle
  6. 6Descending: front wheel coming back down
  7. 7Landing: touches down, slight bounce, back to ride

idle

BUILD LAST
1 frameholdattract screen
Relaxed stand-by between bets. A personality beat, not urgent — a static hold is genuinely enough here.

  • Transparent background — a real alpha channel, corners actually transparent, not a flattened colour.
  • One uniform frame grid per file — every frame in a sheet is the same width × height; it's sliced by width ÷ frame count.
  • Ground contact stays put — same relative position in every frame, or Ace visibly jumps when poses switch.
  • Aspect near 0.89:1 per frame — any resolution above ~74×83 is fine, just keep proportions consistent across all 8 files.
  • Rim light, one consistent side — matches the world's low-sun lighting so he doesn't look pasted onto the background.
